The Travel and Tourism Team has selected the Harvard Business Review article, “AirBnB is Facing anExistential Expansion Problem,” as a work that will inspire the content of the final project. The article utilizesdata pulled from the AirBnB site, as well as data visualizations, to explain the impact of AirBnB rentals onlocal communities in popular tourist destinations, including Lisbon, Portugal; Reykjavik, Iceland; and JoshuaTree, California.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

What is the prevalence of hosts with multiple rental properties in popular tourist locations? What might be the impact on local communities?

How does the prevalence of AirBnB rentals impact local economies, specifically the hospitalitysector?

Will the effects of AirBnB expansion be sustainable given similar questions in the hotel industry?

 
 
 
 

Impact 1: Revenue and Communities via Tourism Clustering

Peiro-Signes, A., Segarra-Ona, M., Miret-Pastor, L., & Verma, R. (2015). The effect of tourismcl usters on U.S. hotel performance. Cornell Hospitality Quarterly, 56(2), 155-167.“The effect of tourism clusters on U.S. hotel performance” presents research on the effect of tourist clusterson hotel performance. Findings here could translate to AirBnB’s tourist clustering as well and give us insightinto the success of such clusters. The idea that AirBnB poses a cultural threat to cities that have touristclusters present could be premature if such clusters do not prove successful. Their model of Revenue perRoom = β₁Chain + β₂Franchise + β₃Luxury + β₄Upscale + β₅Midprice + β₆Economy + β₇Urban +β₈Suburban + β₉Airport + β₁ₒInterstate + β₁₁Resort + β₁₂Cluster + E might translate to AirBnB, and someof these variables will be worth investigating.

 
 
 
 
 
 

Impact 2: Travel Patterns

The beginning of the HBR article presents a notion found in other research articles, namely that AirBnBpresents to its customers the idea that they might become locals, if only for the duration of their stay. Iis P.Tussyadiah and Juho Pesonen found that this notion, cast in terms of “social interaction” has a statisticallysignificant impact on AirBnB’s client-base in choosing peer-to-peer accommodations, alongsidecost-savings. They found no significance in the usual demographic statistics, but suggest looking at“cognitive, psychographic perspectives, such as values, lifestyle, and attitudes” which could be found via textanalysis of reviews.

 
 
 
 
 

Impact 3: local hospitality business

This article provides both a client (survey) and provider (12 executive interviews) perspective on the impactof Airbnb on the hotel industry. The executives do not describe a large impact from Airbnb and theconsensus is that Airbnb caters to a different market segment. The survey covers travelers that have usedand those that have not used Airbnb and examines the different expectations from each group. The areaswith the most significant differences (from ANOVA testing) are security, housekeeping, and loyaltyprograms.

 
 
 

The growth of Airbnb not only impacts the local residents, but also disrupts the businesses of hotel industry.The valuation of Airbnb is $10 billion from the FactSet of Wall Street Journal, and its ranking is only behindthree major public hotel companies, which are Hilton ($21.9 billion), Marriott ($15.9 billion), and Starwood($15 billion). The investment groups even predicted that Airbnb would stack up soon against these leadinghotel chains since the younger generation tends to find cheaper rooms with larger spaces, and thehomeowners incline to make a new source of income. It would eventually cause a high unemployment rateof the hotel companies, and a loss of tax income of the local governments.
